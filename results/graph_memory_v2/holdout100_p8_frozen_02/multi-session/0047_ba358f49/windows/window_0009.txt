Malaysia’s Air Pollution Index (API) is calculated using the concentration of carbon monoxide (CO), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), ozone (O3),
sulphur dioxide (SO2), particulate matter (PM) with a diameter finer
than 10 μm and Particulate Matter <2.5 μm (PM2.5) (APIMS 2019).
Despite the present utilization of PM2.5 to denote the API level in
Malaysia (APIMS 2020), PM10 sourced from vehicles’ soot, biomass
burning, and industrial emission remain a threat to health (Khan et al.,
2016). This is due to its small aerodynamic particulate diameter, which
can easily penetrate the lungs and respiratory system, resulting in
adverse health effects (Tella and Balogun, 2021).
The impact of exposure to PM10 has long be discussed in several
literature to have caused cardiovascular diseases, respiratory diseases,
asthma, birth defects, cerebrovascular health problem, global warming,
rise in hospitalization, absenteeism in school, mortality, morbidity and
its potentiality of being carcinogenic (Bergstrom ¨ et al., 2017; Wang
et al., 2018; Feng et al., 2019). Further, particulate matter is a causative
factor of haze in Malaysia which affects the socio-economic developments, tourism sector, school activities, and cardiovascular
system of both young and old (Song et al., 2019).
There has been a rise in the concentration of PM10 in Malaysia’s
atmosphere due to frequent vehicular emission, industrial emission, and
urbanization (Althuwaynee et al., 2020). Thus, the high concentration of
ambient PM10 has been a major environmental concern that requires
quick intervention. One of the requirements of effective air quality
management is the capability to accurately delineate potential hotspots
of the PM10 concentration for preemptive monitoring and timely control measures. Reliable estimation of the PM10 hazard level will aid
mitigation strategy by offering timely information to stakeholders,
decision-makers, and governments to curtail the impacts of air pollution
(Fortelli et al., 2016). Considering that poor air quality is projected to
increase due to unplanned population growth and socio-economic activities (She et al., 2017), studies that aim to advance ongoing initiatives
to accurately predict future PM10 concentration leveraging advancements in geospatial technology and digitalization are imperative.

impact of PM10 in Malaysia. However, according to the review of

regression methods, while only 12% used non-linear machine learning
techniques. For instance, Juneng et al. (2009) examined the

to produce the principal component spatial loadings in determining the

One of the effective methods to overcome these limitations is machine learning techniques which are not restricted by the non-linear
problem and the complexity of the relationship between the predictors

density model.
Having confirmed the remarkable performance of machine learning

models: Random Forest (RF), K-Nearest Neighbour (KNN), and Naive
Bayes (NB) to predict PM10 spatial hazard hotspots. To the best of the
authors knowledge, these three models have not been used to predict air
pollution comparatively, making it the first of its kind in Malaysia.
Furthermore, the inclusion of spatial modelling enhances the approach
adopted in this study. Therefore, the objectives of this study are (i)

importance of the predictors (iii) comparison of the models in the prediction of PM10 hotspot.
